Our Origin Story.

Established in 2024, Tesseract was formed with the purpose of utilising today's technologies for subgroups in society excluded from the exciting technological innovations of our modern era. Brought together in university, we realised that we each shared a compassion for technology with positive impact as well as an aptitude for software engineering, hence it was only natural to combine these traits and start a business that could harness both passions for societal good. We began to consider technologically neglected communities, specifically those with needs we felt unmet by the current level of devices at their disposal. After considering many marginalised and vulnerable subgroups, we highlighted the Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) learning community.

Within this sector, most toys and educational tools are simplistic; designed purely for resistance to damage and to be cost effective due to general lack of funding for SEND children e.g. featureless dolls, building blocks, plush toys etc. Another popular product for this community consists of sensory devices, designed for giving children sensory stimulation with items like lights and textures. Most sensory devices seem to only target one of the child's senses and are limited in what various interactivity they can have e.g. sensory lights with a single mode, one-style fidget devices etc.

Due to these mundane devices, educators can sometimes struggle to retain a child's attention to their communication or learning task. We’re addressing this

problem with the use of technology to grasp and retain their attention for education. By harnessing the children’s need for sensory stimulation with modern technology, we can enable educators to deliver lessons and communicative exercises with greater ease than traditional methods, while simultaneously allowing the child to have fun while communicating their thoughts, feelings and desires

We believe children with additional needs are also deserving of exciting, cutting-edge devices to the same capability that neurotypical children are provided; devices that are multipurpose for fun and education, ergonomic and aesthetic, and that are suitable to their needs. It’s from this belief that we have designed TouchTalk.

Identifying Our Target Market Within the SEND Community.

To effectively design a product that would meet our objectives, we had to establish our target market. The SEND community is a vast and dynamic community, hence to attempt to design a single product to aid and entertain all of those under its many sub communities wouldn’t be viable. We broke this task down into the following considerations:


Within SEND there are 4 main sub-communities defined by the SEND code of practice (2015,DoE): Communication and Interaction, Cognition and Learning, Social/Emotional and mental health difficulties, and Sensory and/or Physical needs. After researching each of these subgroups and their respective needs and existing devices, we settled on Communication and Interaction. Communication and Interaction includes children and young people with speech, language and communication needs (SLCN) have difficulty in communicating with others. This can typically include children and young people with ASD(Autism Spectrum Disorder), who are likely to have particular difficulties with social interaction. For these children, work is done by educators to provide mediums for communication, such as communication boards and cards, to enable them to express themselves and interact. We have designed TouchTalk to be another medium for which Non-Verbal (NV) children can utilise to communicate in a fun and engaging manner.

As previously mentioned, sensory toys are used extensively for children with ASD. These can involve devices that use lights, noises and even physical textures. Understanding the importance of the uses of these devices is paramount to our product, and hence incorporating them to provide suitable sensory stimulation while producing a unique product to market is our goal. Witnessing the popularity of visual stimulation devices for these children, as well as being aware of the continual improvements of new age projection technologies, we knew we wanted to incorporate a visual projection for TouchTalk. Establishing the level of technological resources required to provide suitable visual projection that could not only provide educational content but keep our product price competitive for the market was key. With these considerations, TouchTalk has implemented a visual projector- capable of projecting light onto any surface to display educational communicative visuals. This visual projection, combined with physical stimulation in the form of varying textures surrounding the actual product, allows TouchTalk to deliver dual sensory stimulation while still remaining cost effective and producing educational content.

The limited government funding for SEND constrains our products production cost to a minimum in order to ensure its viability as a mass purchased product for the sector. This consideration has made us conscious of our production methods and materials, while balancing the cost of resources for our technological features and capabilities. With this in mind, TouchTalk has been designed to provide entertainment and educational material while remaining a durable and long lasting product. We have achieved durability and low manufacturing costs by utilising 3D printing manufacturing methods with hard-wearing rubber filament to ensure our device can withstand the SEND classroom environment.

Within Communication and Interaction, a key exercise performed is conversing with communication boards. These boards consist of multiple simple designed cards which educators will use to ask children questions and observe their answers. They are incredibly common and used sometimes with sign language or makaton, but educators can sometimes struggle to keep a child sufficiently engaged with these boards. As the work being done on providing a communicative medium to these children is prioritised, TouchTalk has been created with the purpose of acting as an additional medium to communication cards. While not designed to completely replace the traditional medium, we aimed to allow the device to act as another option for educators when needing additional attention on the communicative task by engaging the children with light projections of the cards and allowing them to point or touch their designed symbol as another way to express their thoughts and intentions. Not only would TouchTalk enable continued communication between educators and individuals in the class, it holds the attention and wonder of these children for longer and can allow these conversations to last longer than if using traditional methods.

Identifying the UK Special Needs Schools.

As part of our comprehensive market analysis, we have also compiled an interactive map detailing the locations of special needs schools across the UK. This map serves as a valuable resource, providing insights into the distribution of potential users for TouchTalk.
